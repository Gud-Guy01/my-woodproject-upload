Shear stress in timber occurs when forces act parallel to the grain direction, causing one layer of fibers to slide over another along a plane parallel to the grain. This type of failure is particularly critical in notched beams, connections, and lap joints. \\[3mm]
\noindent Wood is very strong when you pull or crush it along its length, but surprisingly weak when forces try to slide one layer of fibers over another horizontally along the grain, because only the weak lignin glue between fibers is resisting it. This makes shear parallel to the grain the sneaky weakness of timber — a beam can look perfectly fine carrying its load, then suddenly split horizontally at a vulnerable point like a notch or connection with absolutely no warning. That is why engineers must always specifically check shear in timber design, especially anywhere the geometry changes suddenly, because that is where timber will silently choose to fail.
